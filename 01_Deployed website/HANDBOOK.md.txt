0 1  /  T H E  B R A N D  I D E A
Why Daftar exists.
Daftar exists to deliver rigorous finance, without Big Four overhead — by doing the work ourselves and standing behind every number.
That sentence is the spine. Everything else — the typography, the colour, the way we talk — flows from it.
Daftar Advisory is a practice of one. One experienced practitioner who actually does the work — who opens the Excel file, reads the contracts, writes the memo, and stands behind the number. No pyramid team. No outsourced execution. No layer of analysts between the reader and the judgement.
Every piece of content must be true to that. No stock photos of handshakes. No "leveraging synergies." No fake team photos. No AI-generated anything pretending to be human.

T H R E E  P R O M I S E S  T O  E V E R Y  R E A D E R
1.	Real craft. We show working papers, formulas, actual numbers (sanitised). We don't hide behind jargon.
2.	Plain language. If a sentence needs decoding, we rewrite it. IFRS is already complicated enough.
3.	No time-wasting. Every post earns its place. If it's not useful, interesting, or true — we don't post it.
 
0 2  /  V O I C E  &  T O N E
How we sound.
If a sentence wouldn't sound natural coming from a senior auditor explaining something over coffee, it doesn't belong.
That's the test. Run every headline, caption, and email through it before publishing. The voice is direct, confident, quietly funny, and always rooted in real work. Not a consulting slide deck. Not a LinkedIn influencer. Not corporate cheerful.

0 3  /  T Y P O G R A P H Y
Two Latin fonts. One Arabic.
Three fonts carry everything — Fraunces for Latin display and body, JetBrains Mono for Latin labels, IBM Plex Sans Arabic for Arabic. Don't add a fourth. Don't substitute similar fonts. Don't use system defaults in a pinch — download the real files and keep them on your desktop.
If a third Latin font appears in the design, the design is wrong.
0 3 . 1  ·  D I S P L A Y  &  L A T I N  B O D Y  —  F R A U N C E S
Fraunces is a variable serif with real personality. Headlines, pull quotes, service names, big numbers, and anything that should feel considered. The italic is our signature — it's how Daftar announces itself on the page.
Rigorous finance, without the overhead.
Download: fonts.google.com/specimen/Fraunces (free, open-source). Weights we use: 300 (light) for headlines · 400 (regular) for body · italic for accent words.
0 3 . 2  ·  L A B E L S  &  D A T A  —  J E T B R A I N S  M O N O
The monospace voice. Tiny labels, post numbers (01 / 09), service codes (S / 01), timestamps, tags, footers, and any time a number needs to feel engineered. The typographic equivalent of a pencil mark on a working paper.
Download: fonts.google.com/specimen/JetBrains+Mono (free, open-source). Weights we use: 400 (regular) — one weight only. Don't bold mono.

Modern Naskh-influenced humanist sans. Free, OFL-licensed, designed by Bold Monday for IBM. Reads with the same quiet authority as PwC's Arabic system across the Middle East — restrained, modern, professional — without the corporate-anonymous feel of defaulting to Helvetica Arabic.

0 3 . 4  ·  T H R E E  A R A B I C  F O N T S  W E  C O N S I D E R E D
Every Arabic typeface decision is also a brand decision. Here's what was on the table and why each one fell short.
▪	Amiri — Beautiful classical Naskh. Reads as Quranic, religious, manuscript-traditional. Daftar is a working practice, not an illuminated text. The handbook's original V1.0 placeholder — replaced.
▪	Cairo — Modern geometric sans, popular and competent. Reads generic — the Arabic equivalent of using Open Sans for a brand that wants to feel considered. No editorial gravity.
▪	Tajawal — Clean, friendly, well-made — but built for product UI, not editorial body text. Doesn't carry the same weight Fraunces brings to Latin headlines.
IBM Plex Sans Arabic wins because it's the only one that reads as considered — engineered, professional, slightly serious — without slipping into either tradition or genericism.
0 3 . 5  ·  H I E R A R C H Y  R U L E S
▪	Headlines are always Fraunces (Latin) or IBM Plex Sans Arabic (Arabic). Never mono. Never sans-serif Latin.
▪	Italic Fraunces in accent rust is reserved for the one word you want the reader to remember — in Latin only. Arabic has no italic.
▪	Monospace is always uppercase, always small (10–14pt), always muted grey or accent rust. Latin labels only — Arabic has no monospace tradition; don't fake one.
▪	Latin body text is Fraunces regular, ink black, 14–18pt. Arabic body is Plex Sans Arabic 400 regular, ink black, 14–18pt.
▪	Never mix more than two sizes in the same paragraph.
0 3 . 6  ·  A R A B I C  —  S A M E  H I E R A R C H Y ,  M I R R O R E D
Arabic doesn't have italic. The accent rule still works — it just becomes colour-only instead of colour-plus-slant. The eye still lands on the word you'd underline.
W E I G H T S
▪	300 — Light. Headlines and display, 28–48pt. Mirrors Fraunces 300 in Latin.
▪	400 — Regular. Body text, 14–18pt. Reading weight.
▪	500 — Medium. Replaces italic for emphasis. Use it sparingly — once per paragraph at most.
T H E  A C C E N T  R U L E ,  I N  A R A B I C
One rust word per headline. Same as Latin — the word you'd underline with a pen. No slant (Arabic has no italic), just colour. If you find yourself colouring two words, recolour one of them ink and try again.
N U M E R A L S
Arabic-Indic ( ٠١٢٣٤٥٦٧٨٩ ) for fully Arabic posts where the audience is Arabic-first. Western Arabic ( 0123456789 ) for mixed-language posts and any post where international readers are part of the audience. Pick one per post and stay consistent. Never mix the two systems in the same headline.
A L I G N M E N T
Arabic body and headlines are always right-aligned. Mixed-language posts align to the dominant language. Same hierarchy as Latin — just mirrored.
 
0 4  /  C O L O U R  S Y S T E M
Five colours. No more.
Paper 70%. Ink 25%. Rust 5%. Never invert these ratios.
Everything else in this section is a consequence of that ratio. Paper dominates the canvas. Ink carries the content. Rust is the 5% that makes the eye land. The restraint is the brand.

S W A T C H E S
Paper	#F4F1EA	The background. Warm offwhite. Never pure white.
Paper Dark	#EBE6DA	Secondary surface. Card backgrounds, subtle contrast zones.
Ink	#1A1814	Primary text. Near-black but warm. Never pure black.
Muted	#78726A	Labels, captions, metadata. Reads as grey but has warmth.
Accent Rust	#A8341F	The one hero colour. Italic words, rules, dots, highlights.

R U L E S  O F  C O L O U R
1.	Accent is precious. If everything is highlighted, nothing is. Rust gets one or two words per post — the word you'd underline with a pen.
2.	Never introduce new colours. No blue for "professional." No green for "finance." No gold for "premium." The restraint is the brand.
3.	Black-and-white fallback. If you're ever forced to print or share in monochrome, the design still holds because contrast does the work.

0 6  /  T H E  G R I D
Space is a feature.
Daftar is built on generous whitespace. If a post feels crowded, it's wrong — delete something. The editorial feel comes from what you leave out, not what you put in.
S Q U A R E  P O S T  C A N V A S
▪	Dimensions: 1080 × 1080 px (Instagram native)
▪	Safe margins: 60px on all sides — nothing touches the edge
▪	Content area: 960 × 960 px inside the margins
▪	Top rule: thin 1px line under the micro wordmark and post number
▪	Bottom rule: same — meta info sits between the two rules
V E R T I C A L  S T R U C T U R E  ( E V E R Y  P O S T )
1.	Top strip (60px): Wordmark left · post number right (01 ◆ 09)
2.	Content zone (varies): The actual post — headline, quote, grid, whatever
3.	Bottom strip (60px): Micro-meta left · @handle right
A L I G N M E N T
▪	Latin: left-align almost everything. Centred text is reserved for the credo/CTA posts only.
▪	Arabic: right-align body and headlines. Mixed-language posts align to the dominant language.
▪	Headlines sit flush left (or flush right in Arabic) with no indent.
▪	Long lines break early — Fraunces and Plex Sans Arabic at big sizes both want room to breathe.
G R I D - B R E A K I N G
One post in every nine can break the grid deliberately — centred typography, asymmetric layout, a single huge number. Use it sparingly. The surprise only works when the rest is disciplined.
 
0 7  /  P O S T  A N A T O M Y
The five parts.
Consistency is what makes Daftar recognisable before the logo is read.
Every Daftar post — regardless of format or language — has the same five structural parts. Learn them once, and you can produce a post in 15 minutes instead of an hour.

0 1  ·  W O R D M A R K  S T R I P
Latin micro wordmark top-left (or Arabic micro top-right for Arabic-led posts), post number opposite. Thin rule underneath. Always the same. Never decorated.
0 2  ·  S E C T I O N  L A B E L
A monospace kicker in accent rust: "03 / SERVICES" or "08 / BEHIND THE DESK." This tells the reader what kind of post they're looking at before they read the headline.
0 3  ·  H E A D L I N E
Fraunces (Latin) or Plex Sans Arabic Light (Arabic), 60–108pt, one or two sentences max. One word in rust — italic if Latin, colour-only if Arabic. The word you want the reader to remember.
0 4  ·  B O D Y
The actual content: a list, a grid, a table, a quote, a stat. This is the "what" — kept as minimal as possible.
0 5  ·  F O O T E R  S T R I P
Context left (e.g. "FIXED SCOPE · FIXED FEE"), handle right. Same as the wordmark strip but inverted. Closes the frame.

0 9  /  W R I T I N G  C A P T I O N S
The 4-beat caption.
The image does the heavy lifting. The caption exists to add context, start a conversation, and give the algorithm something to index. Every Daftar caption follows the same 4-beat structure.
B E A T  ·  H O O K
One sharp sentence that earns the second sentence. No "Hey guys." No emoji rain. Just a sentence that makes someone pause their scroll.
B E A T  ·  C O N T E X T
Two or three sentences that expand the idea. This is where your actual point lives. Write it like you're explaining it to one specific person — not "my audience."
B E A T  ·  T A K E A W A Y
The thing you want the reader to remember. One sentence. If the caption had to shrink to a tweet, this is what would survive.
B E A T  ·  C A L L
A single action: save this, DM "SCOPE," share with a junior auditor, book a call. One call per post. Never two.
If you can't identify the Hook or the Takeaway instantly, the caption isn't finished.
L E N G T H  R U L E S
▪	Minimum: 40 words. Anything shorter reads as lazy.
▪	Maximum: 180 words. Anything longer gets truncated and ignored.
▪	Sweet spot: 80–120 words — enough to say something, short enough to finish.
 
0 9 . 1  ·  B I L I N G U A L  P O S T S  —  T W O  L A N G U A G E S ,  O N E  C O M P O S I T I O N
W H E N  T O  P U B L I S H  I N  A R A B I C
Default: English-only. Most Daftar content — IFRS-technical posts, audit-craft, CPA-journey, lessons from EY and PwC — lives natively in English in MENA finance and travels globally that way. Add Arabic when the topic is GCC-regulatory (ZATCA waves, Saudi TP, SOCPA-specific guidance) or the audience is Arabic-first (KSA family-office CFOs, Jordan SME founders). Skip Arabic when you don't have time to write it natively — half-translated Arabic is worse than no Arabic at all.
O R D E R
Arabic first when the post topic is GCC-regulatory (ZATCA, Saudi TP, SOCPA) or when the audience is dominantly Arabic. English first for IFRS technical posts, audit-craft posts, and any content that's likely to travel internationally. Pick one per post — never run them side-by-side in two columns.
T H E  R U L E  B E T W E E N
نصّ عربي. مكتوب أصلاً بالعربيّة، ليس ترجمة.
—————
English text. Written natively. Not a translation of the Arabic.
Five em dashes (—————), centred, in rust. Not a horizontal line. Not a rule across the full width. The five dashes are the visual handoff — short enough to feel intentional, long enough to register.
T H E  T R A N S L A T I O N  R U L E
Don't machine-translate. Don't translate at all, in fact. Write each version natively to the language. The English version may say something the Arabic doesn't, and vice versa — that's correct. The two versions are siblings, not copies.
L E N G T H  I N  B I L I N G U A L  P O S T S
Each language follows the caption length rules independently — minimum 40 words, maximum 180, sweet spot 80–120. The combined caption can run up to 300 words because the algorithm reads each language separately.

1 1  /  P O S T I N G  C A D E N C E
Rhythm over volume.
Three posts per week, forever. Not four. Not seven. Three. The algorithm rewards consistency far more than volume, and burnout kills more finance IG accounts than anything else.

M O N	Educational (E or F)	Myth-buster or numbered advice. Starts the week with value — highest save rate.
W E D	Proof or craft (D or H)	Big stat or behind-the-desk. Builds credibility mid-week.
F R I	Voice or brand (A, B, G)	Manifesto, list, or credo. Leaves the follower with a feeling heading into the weekend.

B E S T  P O S T I N G  T I M E S  ( A M M A N  /  G C C  A U D I E N C E )
▪	Morning: 8:00–9:00 AM — commuters, morning coffee scroll
▪	Evening: 7:00–9:00 PM — after-work scroll, highest engagement window in MENA
▪	Avoid: Friday mornings (prayer) and late nights after 11 PM
S T O R I E S  V S  F E E D  P O S T S
▪	Feed: the 3 posts per week above. These are the brand.
▪	Stories: daily or every-other-day. Behind-the-scenes, polls, quick takes, reshares. Lower stakes, higher frequency.
▪	Never move feed-quality content to stories. Stories disappear — your grid is forever.
 
1 2  /  W H A T  W E  N E V E R  P O S T
The refusal list.
Refusals are what protect the brand when reach, ego, or pressure creep in.
The things you refuse to do shape a brand more than the things you do.

1 2 . 1  ·  G E N E R A L  R E F U S A L S
▪	Client names or sanitised client work without written permission. Audit confidentiality is permanent. If in doubt, don't.
▪	Motivational quotes over stock photos. Not our brand. Not our voice. Not ever.
▪	"Hustle culture" content. We're selling craft and judgement, not burnout.
▪	Hot takes on politics or religion. We work across MENA, GCC, and the US — neutrality is a commercial asset.
▪	Bashing other firms by name. You can critique Big 4 overhead as a category. Never name and shame a specific firm or partner.
▪	AI-generated imagery pretending to be real. Hand-drawn diagrams and real screenshots only. This is a craft practice.
▪	Inspirational humblebrags. "Can't believe I landed this huge client" — no.
▪	Ultra-generic advice. "Work hard, stay humble" adds nothing. If a post could be about any profession, it's not a Daftar post.
▪	Emoji storms. One or two per caption, maximum. Geometric only — ▪ ◆ ▣ — never the festive ones.
▪	Promises about outcomes. Never "save 40% on your audit" or "guaranteed pass." This is a regulated-adjacent practice. Overpromising is career-ending.
 
1 2 . 2  ·  A R A B I C - S P E C I F I C  R E F U S A L S
Same logic as 12.1. The things we refuse to do in Arabic shape the brand more than the things we do.
▪	Machine translation. Google Translate is a starting point at best. Always rewrite. If we don't have time to write Arabic natively, we publish English-only that week.
▪	Decorative Naskh or Diwani fonts. These belong on wedding invitations and book covers, not on a finance practice's working voice.
▪	Arabic translation of the avoided English words. If we don't say "leverage" in English, we don't say نُوظِّف in Arabic. The avoidance list applies in both languages.
▪	Religious framing. Phrases like بإذن الله or ما شاء الله in business posts — even when authentic to the writer — read as performative on a professional feed. Save them for personal accounts.
▪	Mixing numeral systems. If a post uses ٢٠٢٦ (Arabic-Indic), it can't also use 2026 (Western). One per post.
▪	Forced bilingualism. Not every post needs Arabic. Audit-technical content (deferred tax, IFRS 15 disaggregation, lease modifications) lives natively in English in the MENA finance world. Forcing Arabic on it sounds awkward to readers and dilutes the post.
